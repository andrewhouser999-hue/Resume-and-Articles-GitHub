There is a manager in almost every organization who is very good at solving problems. Her team knows it. So they bring her their problems. She solves them quickly, efficiently, and well. Her numbers are solid. Her team likes her. Her calendar is full.

She is also, without knowing it, sending her people the same message every single day: *you are not the solution source. I am.*

Say that long enough and people believe it.

---

The fix reflex is one of the most common and most costly habits in management. It feels like leadership. It produces results in the short term. And it is quietly dismantling the professional identity of the people around you.

Every time a manager solves a problem her team member could have worked through — given time, given support, given the right question — she has done something that looks helpful and is subtly harmful. She has taken an identity-forming moment and converted it into a dependency moment. The team member doesn't just leave without a solution. He leaves with a slightly more confirmed belief that the manager is the capable one and he is not.

That belief compounds quietly. Over months, over years, it produces teams that execute well and think poorly. The manager who fixes fastest is often the one whose team develops slowest. The bottleneck isn't the team's capability. It's what they have been taught to believe about it.

---

Adam Grant's research on organizational dynamics consistently shows this pattern: managers who locate their value in having answers create teams oriented around extracting those answers. Managers who locate their value in growing the people around them create teams oriented around developing their own judgment. The first team performs while the manager is present. The second performs after she has left the room.

The distinction is not about being helpful versus hands-off. It is about what your help communicates about the person receiving it.

Fixing says: here is the answer.
Developing says: here is how I think about this — now you try.

One closes the loop. The other builds an identity.

---

When I was asked to build a communication training program for new financial associates, the instinct most people bring to that assignment is addition: what else can we teach? I started from a different question — why isn't the existing training producing the results the organization needs?

The answer wasn't a skills gap. The new associates had information. They had scripts. They had product knowledge. They were underperforming not because they lacked tools but because they didn't yet see themselves as professionals being trained to a world-class standard.

The program I built framed the work as an invitation into a professional community being trained at a standard the industry rarely offers. The cohorts that went through it averaged 30% more perfect-score client interactions than every previous class. Several of those associates went on to carry the highest perfect-score percentages in their cohort. Not because I gave them a better script. Because they accepted the picture of who they were becoming.

Skills training has a ceiling. Identity doesn't.

---

C.S. Lewis wrote that you cannot help people permanently by doing for them what they could and should do for themselves. The mechanism here is precise: developing people requires believing something about them before they believe it about themselves. It requires holding a picture of their capability steady while they are still forming it. That is what changes people — not the technique, not the framework, not the feedback loop. The belief the manager carries into the room about who is sitting across from her.

That is not a management skill. It is a posture toward human potential. And it is the thing that separates a manager who builds people from one who simply uses them.

---

The managers who consistently build high-performing teams are not the ones with the best answers. They are the ones whose people leave conversations believing they are more capable than when they walked in.

That metric is harder to track. It shows up later. And it is the only one that compounds.

The question worth asking is not: how do I solve this faster? It is: what am I communicating about this person every time I intervene — and is it true?
