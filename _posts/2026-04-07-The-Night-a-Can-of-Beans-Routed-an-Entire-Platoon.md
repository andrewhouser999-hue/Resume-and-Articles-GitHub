title: "The Night a Can of Beans Routed an Entire Platoon"
date: 2026-04-07
author: Andrew J Houser
---

We had a rare gift that day — free time. A small group of us spent it the way soldiers do: pushing equipment to its limits across terrain no sane civilian would attempt. We discovered that HMMWVs will drive underwater and that a deuce-and-a-half will back up a near-vertical embankment. I can confirm the latter personally, having come face-to-face with one around a blind corner on a narrow trail. The shock on our faces apparently made for good entertainment from the cab.

Before the day started, we dug a fire pit — roughly a foot deep and eight feet across — and filled it with branches and debris. By evening it would be a perfect bed of coals. We had plans.

By nightfall, those plans had delivered. Steaks came off the grate perfectly. Cold Budweisers materialized. The kind of conversation that only happens between men who have spent a day doing something ridiculous together filled the air around the fire. One by one, soldiers peeled off their fatigues and crawled into sleeping bags. I stayed up with my commander through a few more stories before I couldn't hold my eyes open. I nestled my sleeping bag between the edge of the cooling coal pit and the Army personnel carrier and let the warmth put me under.

What followed is burned into my memory more completely than the coals burned through my sleeping bag.

The explosion was everything my training told me it was. My body responded before my mind did. In the fraction of a second between the blast and awareness, I was already moving — directly over the top of the nearest soldier, who was crawling for his life in the same direction. I drove myself down the embankment with the kind of focus that has no room for conscience. That came later.

What emerged from the tree line was not the hardened image the United States Army works hard to project. Pale white skin glowed in the moonlight like a collection of startled ghosts, every one of us standing in nothing but our whitie tighties, confused, breathless, and faintly ridiculous. National Guardsmen, trained and ready, blinking at each other in the dark trying to remember what threat we were responding to. We stood in a loose circle running hands across our torsos checking for shrapnel wounds, which is a strange thing to do when you are also trying not to laugh at the man next to you.

Nothing.

The pit was nearly empty. Feathers from shredded sleeping bags drifted through the air. I reached up and touched my face — small burns from flying coals, nothing serious. The personnel carrier had a new marking across its side, a darkened streak where the heat had scorched the paint. And there, in the center of the pit, sat the unmistakable remains of a large tin can.

The math wasn't hard. A 114-ounce can of Pork and Beans, sealed, dropped into a bed of coals by a commander who had stayed up alone with a nearly empty cooler and what I can only describe as poor judgment dressed up as curiosity.

He began pleading before anyone said a word.

We chided him with the loyalty only that kind of shared experience can produce — thoroughly, affectionately, and without mercy. His reputation survived. The story did not stay quiet.

I still have the sleeping bag scars to prove it. And I remain grateful for the closest thing to combat I ever experienced, the collective grace we extended to our commander, and the best steak I have ever eaten.
